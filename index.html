New York City in Data

A day in the life of a city that never sleeps

By Somaiyah Hafeez

They say New York City is the city of dreams.

From being featured in so many classic Hollywood as well as Bollywood movies, almost everyone who first comes to the city feels like they are living in a film—the Brooklyn Bridge in Kal Ho Na Ho (Tomorrow May Not Come) where Shah Rukh Khan stretches his arms in his quintessential pose; The Godfather’s opening scene shot in Little Island; and the Spiderman scene in Central Park’s Bow Bridge. When you find yourself in these places—they feel strangely familiar and alien at the same time—as if you’re the main character of a movie, as if this was scripted and destined.

But what is it really like living in the Big Apple?

My friend, Bibi Emina, and I moved to the city in August 2024—for about eight months now we have called this city that “never sleeps” our home. But there is so much that the movies do not tell you about the city.

I tried to paint a picture of an average day in our life in the city by turning to data.

The Subway: New York’s Shared Rhythm

While New York City’s unique subway system has been featured countless times in many movies and television series, they do not show the everyday chaos on the trains: from struggling to make your way in a throng of people during the morning and evening rush hours to the cacophony of noises—musicians serenading tired commuters, immigrant vendors selling candy and snacks, the occasional, inevitable fight—and the sharp metallic scream of the train as it barrels into the station.

I do not take the subway everyday for school, Emina does. She commutes for more than an hour to reach her school in Brooklyn.

“The A train, which I take for school, is packed with people until we have reached Chambers or Canal street”, she told me.

Emina rides the A train from 125th Street and Saint Nicholas Avenue to Jay St-Metrotech for her college and to 34th Street for her work.

“I just look around to see if someone is exiting at the next stop to take their seat”, she said when I asked her what she thinks or feels during her long commutes. “I look at people sometimes, when I am not exhausted and listen to their conversation because it is so fun.”

On days when she commutes to school, she takes the A train from 125th Street and Saint Nicholas to Jay St MetroTech in Brookly. Twice a week she commutes to school and to work from there and then back home.

311 Complaints: The City’s Roared Complaints

During the night, I toss and turn in my bed—unable to sleep, mostly due to my own thoughts, but also because of the rap music blasting outside my window and the loud fights that break out on the street corner. I have never filed a noise complaint. Not once. Mostly because I’m timid. Partly because I still feel like a stranger in this city. What rights does an outsider have?

But the city won’t have it. New Yorkers don’t stay quiet. They roar. They scream. They pick up the phone and lodge a complaint.

On March 24th, 2025, the city received thousands of such screams.

In just four zip codes, chosen because those are the neighborhoods we spend our days in, the variety of complaints paints a picture more vivid than any film set could capture.

In 10027, where we live, there were 12 noise complaints, 13 about parking, and 13 more about heat. Emina, like me, doesn't like how noisy the neighborhood can get. When I told her about the 311 complaints, she said she didn't know that one can file a complaint about noise but maybe she will file one now.

In 10025, just a few blocks south and where I go to school, heat complaints were the loudest at 16, but noise was not far behind at 12.

Parking complaints dominated in Brooklyn neighborhood where Emina goes to school, at 19 complaints--the highest in these categories.

The movies don’t show the heat complaints or the aching feet after a long commute. They also skip the 311 calls and the tossing and turning because of the noise outside your apartment. But maybe that’s what makes this city real.
